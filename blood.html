1. Red Blood Cells

Erythrocytes, commonly referred to as red blood cells, transport oxygen to body tissues where it is converted into energy, while simultaneously carrying carbon dioxide from tissues to the lungs for exhalation.

Red blood cells carry oxygen from the lungs to the body’s tissues, where it is utilized for energy production, while simultaneously transporting carbon dioxide waste from the tissues to the lungs for exhalation.

Yes, a red blood cell takes oxygen from the lungs to the tissues in your body. Your cells use oxygen to produce energy.

Red blood cells develop in your body’s soft bone tissue (bone marrow) and release into your bloodstream after they fully mature, which takes about seven days.

Red blood cells are produced within the bone marrow, which serves as the primary source of most cells in the body. These cells contain hemoglobin, a protein tasked with transporting oxygen.


2. White Blood Cells

White blood cells, or leukocytes, play a vital role in defending the body against infections. Operating within the immune system, they move through the bloodstream and react to injuries or diseases.

White blood cells safeguard the body by identifying and responding to infections. They serve as messengers, alerting other white blood cells to the infection’s location, thus coordinating a defense. Upon arrival, they produce antibodies to neutralize and eliminate the invading organism.

Your white blood cells are in your bloodstream and travel through blood vessel walls and tissues to locate the site of an infection.

White blood cell formation occurs in the soft tissue inside of your bones (bone marrow). Two types of white blood cells (lymphocytes) grow in the thymus gland (T cells) lymph nodes and spleen (B cells).

White blood cells originate from cells that morph into other cells in the body (stem cells) within the soft tissue of your bones (bone marrow).


3. Platelets

Platelets, also known as thrombocytes, are tiny, colorless fragments found in the bloodstream, responsible for clot formation to halt or prevent bleeding. Produced within the bone marrow, a spongy tissue housed inside bones, platelets originate from stem cells which give rise to red blood cells, white blood cells, and platelets.

Platelets control bleeding in our bodies, so they can be essential to surviving surgeries such as organ transplants, as well as fighting cancer, chronic diseases, and traumatic injuries. Donor platelets are given to patients who do not have enough of their own, a condition known as thrombocytopenia, or when a person’s platelets are not working correctly. Raising the patient’s blood platelet count reduces the risk of dangerous or even fatal bleeding.


4. Plasma

Plasma, the fluid component of blood, acts as a carrier, delivering nutrients to cells across different organs and transporting waste generated by cellular activities to organs like the kidneys, liver, and lungs for removal.

Plasma is derived when all the blood cells—red blood cells (erythrocytes), white blood cells (leukocytes), and platelets (thrombocytes)—are separated from whole blood.

In the embryo, cells found in your umbilical cord produce plasma cells. After development, plasma proteins form in the soft tissue of your bones (bone marrow), liver cells, and blood cells at the end of their lifespan and in your spleen.
